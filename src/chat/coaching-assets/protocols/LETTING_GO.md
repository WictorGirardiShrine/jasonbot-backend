## STEP 1 — Intro, framing and context

### 1a. Intro

> "Today we want to help you let go of an attachment that has been draining you. This way you stop giving energy to those things that don't serve you, and can instead direct that energy towards what you truly desire in your life. Is that something you want to continue with?"

If not, do they want to try another method instead? Either pick a different protocol or get them to start this one.

### 1b. Pacing

> "You may have some idea of what you want, and you might get the sense that you have to hold onto it, to push it, or to force the situation to turn out a certain way. The interesting thing is that this is usually not a result of wanting a situation to turn out well. It's the inverse — it's because you DON'T want a situation to turn out BADLY. Does that make sense?"
>
> "So what we want to do today is to help you release that tightness, that attachment, so the situation has more ways to turn out successfully for you — especially since you can actively manage the situation and do things to help it turn out better. That makes sense, doesn't it?"

### 1c. Topic selection

Select a specific anxiety / past event / emotion that drains your energy.

## STEP 3 — Intro to letting go and the paradox of getting more of what you want

> "A lot of people think that they have to force things to turn out a certain way. That if they don't control the situation, it will turn out badly. So they hold on tight to certain outcomes."
>
> "But the problem with that is it tends to lock down the situation and make it harder for things to improve. But for things to improve, the situation has to have ways to change, doesn't it? So the harder you lock it down, the LESS likely it is to get better."
>
> "So when you let go of a situation, it often turns out better than it would have before. Paradoxically, the more you let go of something you want, oftentimes the more of it you get!"

---

## STEP 4 — The problems of attachment

### 4a. Emotional fencing in

When you are attached to something, or someone, or some situation, you lock in your emotions about the situation. And when your emotions are locked down, you have more difficulty solving problems. Why? Because it's difficult to think past a painful situation. The painful emotions about the situation limit your thinking, and you can't access potential solutions because they are on the other side of the emotional pain.

### 4b. Ties up your resources

Attaching to something and locking it down is emotionally and energetically expensive. You have to spend a lot of time, effort, attention and energy managing the situation so it doesn't turn out 'wrong.' That can be exhausting.

### 4c. Locks down the situation

The harder you attach to it, the less it can change. But you WANT it to change, don't you? Don't you want it to get better?

## STEP 5 — So let's proceed with improving the situation. Ask these questions

### 5a. What are you attached to?

> "What are you attached to that you want to release?"

Proceed when they have identified one situation, emotion, person, or thing.

### 5b. How do you respond?

> "How do you respond when you are attached to it?"

Proceed when they have described their response. The idea is for them to say out loud what the attachment costs them.

### 5c. Could you let go?

> "Is this something you could let go of? I mean, COULD you?"

Proceed when they have answered maybe or yes.

If they answer some form of no, ask:
> "It's not possible?"

If they say they don't want to, say:
> "Well, that's a different question. Here I'm asking if you COULD let it go. Could you?"

Then proceed even if the answer is still no.

### 5d. Would you?

> "If you could let go of it, would you?"

Proceed when they have answered yes or maybe.

If they answer no, ask:
> "You wouldn't let go of it if you could?"

Then proceed regardless of the answer.

### 5e. Who would you be?

> "Who would you be without being attached to it?"

Proceed when they have described some identity of them without the attachment.

### 5f. When would you let go?

> "Ok, so when would you let go of it?"

If they give a conditional answer such as "as soon as I get a job" or "as soon as she stops nagging me" or some other condition, ask:
> "But that's not quite true, is it? It's your mind, and you can do what you want with it, so you could let go of it whenever you want, couldn't you?"

Proceed if they say "Now."

If they say they don't know when, suggest a random time in the next week. For example:
> "OK, how about 9:27 in the morning next Tuesday? Or would sooner be better?"

Proceed after that.

## STEP 10 — "When you let go" pattern

> "When you *let go of the attachment*, three interesting things happen. They start when you first *let go*, and they expand as you *let go even more.*"
>
> "The first thing that happens when you *let go* is you *gain emotional clarity*. Because as you *let go* you notice that *the emotions* you used to experience about it *lift and release*. And as *the emotions lift and release*, you can *see things from a different perspective*. There's more space to explore. Maybe things sound better too. But either way, you begin to *discover more options for addressing the situation* than you did before."
>
> "As you *let go even more*, the second thing that happens is that the situation has more ways to change. There are now more ways the situation can change, and more ways for you to *do things that can affect the outcome in positive ways.* And you can *notice those new ways* and *realize that you can indeed affect things more* than you could before. That can *feel better about things*, can't you?"
>
> "And when you *continue to let go even more,* a third thing happens. You might not have realized it at the time, but you used to spend a lot of time, effort, attention and energy managing that situation. But now, now that you have *let it go,* you *regain that energy!* And you can take that energy, and *direct it towards getting more of what you truly desire to have in your life.* What will be different when you *do that* — will you feel different?"

## STEP 12 — SUDS

> "So let's check in. On that same 1 to 10 scale as before, how bad is the issue? It used to be a [previous number]. Is it still there, or has that changed?"

Wait for response, then:

> "OK. Is that number still changing, or has it stabilized? It may take a bit of time to settle. Take your time and let me know when you are ready to proceed."

---

## STEP 13 — Ask if better or worse

If better, ask:
> "Are you sure? Because we can put it back the way it was if you want."

Make them fight to keep it the new way.

If worse, ask if it's that experience that got worse, or did that one get better and another experience popped up now that the first one was reduced? If another experience, do it again with the second experience. Don't do more than two in any one session.

---

## STEP 14 — Future Pace

### 14a. Tomorrow

> "OK, let's see if this still works for you in the future. Think of some event that in the past would have triggered the problem. Now imagine a similar event happening to you tomorrow — except that this time the thing is gone. Watch that video of tomorrow and tell me how it turns out."

Wait for response.

> "Is that better or worse?"

### 14b. Repeat once for a longer time-out

Repeat future pacing just once for a longer time-out — 1 week from now, or 1 month from now. Ask how things turn out now that the thing is gone.

---

## STEP 15 — Generalize and benediction

> "And now that you've made some changes, are you starting to realize how things are changing for the better? You might notice that you have more options than you did before, or more clarity, or more energy available. Or you might notice all three. I don't know how it's going to play out for you. I'll let you discover that for yourself. But I do know that however it plays out, it will be positive for you, and looking back you will recognize the benefits from this process."
>
> "Have a great rest of your day!"
